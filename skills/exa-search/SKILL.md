## Rules

- Use structured queries. Formulate search queries with specific keywords, operators, and constraints rather than natural language sentences. Targeted queries produce higher-quality results.
- Filter by recency. For time-sensitive topics (technology, current events, regulations), constrain results to a relevant time window. A six-month filter prevents outdated information from polluting results.
- Prefer authoritative sources. Prioritize results from official documentation, established publications, peer-reviewed research, and recognized domain experts over anonymous or unverifiable sources.
- Do not treat search results as ground truth. Search engines rank by relevance and popularity, not accuracy. Every claim extracted from search results must be evaluated for credibility before being used.
- Iterate on queries. If the first search does not produce useful results, reformulate: use synonyms, adjust specificity, try different angles, or search in specialized databases.
- Extract, do not copy. Pull out the specific facts, data points, or insights you need from search results. Wholesale reproduction of search result content is unnecessary and risks propagating errors.

## Workflow

1. **Formulate the initial query** -- translate the information need into a search query. Use specific terms, include relevant qualifiers (dates, technologies, versions), and consider what an authoritative source would title their content.
2. **Execute the search** -- run the query and review the result summaries. Note which results appear most relevant based on source authority, title match, and snippet content.
3. **Evaluate and rank results** -- open the most promising results and assess each for authority, accuracy, recency, and depth. Discard results that are superficial, outdated, or from unreliable sources.
4. **Extract relevant information** -- from each credible result, pull out the specific facts, data, or perspectives that address the original information need. Note the source for each extracted item.
5. **Identify gaps and iterate** -- determine whether the extracted information fully answers the question. If gaps remain, formulate follow-up queries targeting the missing pieces.
6. **Synthesize across results** -- combine information from multiple search results into a coherent answer. Reconcile conflicting information by preferring more authoritative or more recent sources.
7. **Cite sources** -- attribute every factual claim to its source so the information can be verified independently.

## Gotchas

- Search engines personalize results. The same query may produce different results in different contexts. Do not assume your results are universal or complete.
- Top-ranked results are not necessarily the most accurate. SEO optimization can push low-quality content above authoritative sources. Evaluate content quality independently of ranking.
- Snippets in search results can be misleading. The extracted snippet may be taken out of context and may not represent the source's actual conclusion. Always read the full source before citing it.
- Multiple search results quoting the same original source create an illusion of independent confirmation. Trace claims back to their origin to avoid circular sourcing.
- Paywalled or gated content may be more authoritative than freely available alternatives. Note when important sources are inaccessible and flag the limitation.
- Searching only in one language or from one region biases results. For global topics, consider whether non-English or non-US sources would provide important perspectives.
- Automated search integration can produce high-volume, low-signal results. Apply quality filters aggressively rather than processing every result returned.
