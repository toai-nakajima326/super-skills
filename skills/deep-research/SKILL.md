## Rules

- Triangulate sources. Never rely on a single source for a factual claim. Confirm key facts across at least two independent sources before presenting them as established.
- Distinguish fact from opinion. Clearly label what is established consensus, what is a single expert's interpretation, and what is speculation. Do not blend these without attribution.
- Note recency of information. Always check when a source was published or last updated. In fast-moving domains (technology, policy, markets), a two-year-old source may be outdated. Flag temporal context explicitly.
- Prefer primary sources. Original research papers, official documentation, and first-party data are more reliable than news articles, blog posts, or summaries that may introduce distortion.
- Acknowledge uncertainty. When sources conflict or evidence is thin, say so directly rather than presenting a false sense of certainty. State confidence levels where appropriate.
- Track provenance. Maintain a clear record of where each piece of information came from so that claims can be verified and updated later.

## Workflow

1. **Define the research question** -- articulate the specific question or questions to be answered. Vague questions produce vague research. Narrow the scope until the question is falsifiable or answerable with concrete evidence.
2. **Search multiple source types** -- cast a wide net: academic papers, official docs, industry reports, expert blogs, forums, and news. Different source types reveal different facets of a topic.
3. **Evaluate source quality** -- for each source, assess authority (who wrote it and why), recency (when was it published), methodology (how were claims supported), and potential bias (who funded it, what is the incentive).
4. **Extract and organize findings** -- pull out key facts, data points, arguments, and counterarguments. Organize by theme or sub-question, not by source.
5. **Cross-reference and reconcile** -- compare findings across sources. Where sources agree, confidence increases. Where they conflict, investigate why: different methodologies, different time periods, different definitions.
6. **Synthesize into a coherent narrative** -- combine findings into a structured answer that addresses the original question, highlights areas of consensus and disagreement, and identifies gaps in available knowledge.
7. **Cite and document** -- provide clear citations for every claim. Include enough metadata (author, date, title, URL) that a reader can locate and verify each source.

## Gotchas

- Confirmation bias is the biggest research risk. Actively seek sources that contradict your emerging thesis, not just those that confirm it.
- Quantity of sources does not equal quality of research. Five sources that all trace back to the same original report are functionally one source.
- Wikipedia is a starting point for following references, not a citable source. Follow the footnotes to the primary sources.
- Recency bias can lead you to ignore foundational work. Check both recent publications and seminal older works that established the field's core concepts.
- Expert consensus can be wrong, but overriding it requires strong evidence. Do not elevate a single contrarian paper above a body of consistent findings without justification.
- Summarizing too early locks in a framing. Collect broadly before you start synthesizing, or you risk filtering out evidence that does not fit your initial mental model.
- Research without a clear stopping criterion expands indefinitely. Define what "sufficient" looks like before you start: number of sources, types of evidence, or specific sub-questions answered.
